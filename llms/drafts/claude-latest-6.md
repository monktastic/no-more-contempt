# The Perfect Crime

> _For so long as the root of wickedness is hidden, it is strong. But when it is recognized, it is dissolved. When it is revealed, it perishes. ... It is powerful because we have not recognized it._ — The Gospel of Philip

## Prelude

Once, on a "heroic dose" of psilocybin, I was shown something that shattered my understanding of good and evil. It wasn't a gentle insight—it was a cascading series of revelations, each more horrifying than the last, that demolished my entire worldview and replaced it with something infinitely more mysterious and meaningful.

Two decades later, I'm still reeling from the consequences.

What I saw wasn't just about psychology or morality. It was about the fundamental architecture of evil itself—how it operates, how it spreads, and most disturbingly, how it uses _our own goodness_ as its primary transmission vector. By the end of that night, I understood why every major wisdom tradition has warned about the same thing, using different metaphors: there is something that hijacks human consciousness, turning it against itself. It coordinates across humans, and its most brilliant trick is convincing us it doesn't exist.

I saw how some of civilization's core assumptions—that hating bad people is good; that reality is composed of fundamentally inert "stuff"; that rational thought is our only reliable guide—were themselves vectors of the very darkness we claim to oppose. Not accidental side effects, but essential components of how it spreads.

What follows is an attempt to show you what I saw—and why it matters more urgently than almost anything else we could be paying attention to right now.

By necessity, I'll convey some of this in poetic terms; but there's also a technical component, which may help digest it for those whose minds work like mine. Of course, take everything I say with a grain of salt—it was a psychedelic trip, after all—but also, integral to the insight was that you already know everything I'm about to tell you, whoever you are. You _must_—because at the deepest level, I am you. But I'm getting ahead of myself.

A word about audience: if you need peer-reviewed studies before considering that consciousness might work differently than materialist assumptions suggest, this essay probably isn't for you—at least not yet. But if you've sensed something like this yourself, or if the way we collectively relate to "evil people" has started to feel deeply wrong in ways you can't quite articulate, then read on. This is an attempt to give language to something you may already know.

Throughout this essay, I'll refer to this coordinated system of self-deception as both "the pattern" and "the darkness"—they're the same thing. The pattern is how it operates; the darkness is what it does to reality. Use whichever helps you see it more clearly.

# Part I: The Mechanism

## The Limbo State

Think back to a time when you skirted your conscience—acted against your own deepest values while pretending it was okay. In that moment, part of you knew _perfectly well_ that you were doing wrong, but you shielded that knowledge from conscious awareness, so that it didn't _feel_ wrong. All you could hear were your own clever justifications. Knowledge of your wrongdoing existed in a kind of limbo state, in which you simultaneously _knew_ and yet somehow _didn't._ 

It's a kind of self-deception that operates beneath the threshold of conscious awareness—and it's the secret mechanism behind _all the evil there ever was,_ from the tiniest unkindness to the greatest atrocity. Our examples may differ in degree, but not in kind.

Even the worst person can only cause harm by using exactly this trick to shield their minds from perceiving the _life_ in their victims—that spark that would remind them of themselves. If they could see it, they couldn't do it. This suggests that there's a deeper aspect of mind—call it their _inner Light_—that doesn't want to.

We normally cannot see this part of them—or so we believe. The truth is that part of us *can* see it at all times, but we're strategically hiding it from ourselves for a very surprising reason that I'll try to point out.

## The Perfect Crime

Bring to mind someone you consider truly evil—not Hitler or some abstract monster, but someone real whose actions genuinely horrify you. Maybe a political figure, maybe someone from your own life, maybe someone from the news.

Can you feel that edge where your desire to see justice done crosses into something darker? An urge not just to stop them, but to see them _suffer_?

I certainly can.

I was shown that while this urge felt like it was coming from my authentic goodness, it was actually _feeding_ the very darkness I claimed to oppose. Not accidentally, mind you, but _intentionally_. 

But why? And how?

Every bone in my body needed to believe that such people weren't just different from me, but somehow _fundamentally_ so. Sure, I had done things before that I knew were harmful at the time, but _their_ behavior was something quite different; something alien.

Or was it?

I saw that the thought itself—_you and I are nothing alike_—wasn't a neutral observation, but an encoded threat. I was ostensibly giving them a chance to prove me wrong, while on a deeper level secretly whispering: *I can see that you're lost—so I will pretend to lead you home while making damn sure you never get there.*

That chilling message went so far beyond malicious that it looped around to being... _relieving_. It freed them of the burden of trying to resist their darkness—a struggle every one of us knows all too well. Indeed, it was the very struggle I was confronted with in that moment.

Consciously, I knew that if I lost that struggle, I would license them to lose theirs, thereby creating more evil. I was a "good person" and couldn't let that happen, so I had to do everything in my power to stop it—and yet I so badly wanted to punish them. The solution was obvious.

If I just buried the knowledge of my malevolent intention carefully enough, then it effectively wouldn't exist. And if nobody could tell the difference between my intention and an authentically good one, then how could there *be* any difference? There couldn't!

Therefore, if they *did* embrace their own darkness as a result of my behavior, it couldn't be because of me. It could only be because they're irredeemably evil and unable to appreciate my awesome righteousness.

It was the _perfect_ crime—except for one little detail: part of me would always know the truth.

## How The Message Gets Delivered

How could simply _harboring contempt_ convey such a complex, dynamic, malevolent message?

The answer lies in a paradox of shared knowledge—and it's more technical than you might expect. If it's too hard to follow, don't worry too much about the details.

You see, not only do we know that we're not intrinsically superior to others; they _know_ we know, and we _know_ they know we know, ad infinitum. This is called [_common knowledge_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_knowledge_\(logic\)) in logic and game theory.

It is therefore also common knowledge that _pretending_ to be superior—and punishing them on that basis—is profoundly hypocritical, and will only reinforce their darkness; which we can then blame them for; which they _also_ know we know they know, etc.

When you look at someone with contempt, you're not just being mean. You're eliciting this primal knowledge, knowing that a very deep part of them will receive the malevolent message. This can happen through dozens of subtle channels they perceive subconsciously—your tone, your microexpressions, your word choice, the way you hold your body.

You're saying, in a language that requires no words: "I know we're not fundamentally different. I know this judgment is hypocritical. I know this will drive you deeper into your darkness. And I'm doing it anyway. Good luck."

They receive this message perfectly. Not consciously, but some part of them hears it with perfect clarity.

And they respond—defensively, defiantly, by digging in their heels. And since you "don't know" you were doing this, their response then proves you were right about them, justifying further contempt, in a self-perpetuating spiral.

Watch what happens in your own mind the next time you feel contempt for someone. There's a moment—you might miss it if you're not looking carefully—where part of you knows exactly what you're doing. You know your contempt will make them worse, not better. You know it will drive them deeper into defensiveness. And you know you can blame it on them while hiding it from yourself.

This is the mechanism. And once you see it in yourself, you can't unsee it.

## Why We All Understand Each Other

But how is such common knowledge possible—with complete strangers no less? How do we know not just that we're not fundamentally superior to them, but that they know we know, etc.?

Because we both retain a subtle memory of our common origin.

You can think of this in physicalist terms if you wish—the origin of life, the Big Bang—but this doesn't do it full justice. The truth is stranger and more immediate.

The Life I see in you is the same Life I feel in me. Not just similar, or even merely identical, but somehow the _very same thing._ One Life, experiencing itself from countless vantage points. I know this, you know this, I know you know this—not as intellectual fact, but as lived reality existing beneath conscious awareness.

This is why the message of contempt lands so precisely. This is why the communication works through channels we don't consciously control. We're not really separate beings shouting across a void. We're more like different hands of the same body, pretending not to recognize each other.

I can communicate my darkness to you through contempt, drive you deeper into your own darkness, then honestly claim I was just "trying to help" or "standing up for what's right." Part of me will genuinely believe it.

But another part knows the truth. And that knowledge doesn't simply vanish.

## The Mirror

Life knows when it's lying to itself—and that knowledge gets woven into reality in ways our conscious minds don't track but our lives somehow reflect back.

Think about the last time someone criticized you and your ego got bruised. Maybe later that day you were irritable with someone else—harsher than necessary, more dismissive. You took out your wounded pride on someone who had nothing to do with it. That's how darkness spreads through ordinary psychological mechanisms.

And here's the kicker: when you look at someone with contempt, you're _trying_ to bruise their ego this way, knowing it will make them lash out at others. Part of you knows this. Part of you wants this. Because then they'll prove you were right about them all along.

I kept finding myself in situations where people were subtly manipulative—friends who'd twist situations to look good, partners who'd gaslight me about their intentions, colleagues who'd take credit while deflecting blame. And every time, I'd feel righteous indignation. "Why do I keep attracting these people?"

Until the day I saw it: I was doing exactly the same things. Twisting situations to maintain my self-image as a good person. Gaslighting _myself_ about my real intentions. Taking credit for good outcomes while deflecting blame for bad ones. The people I kept "attracting" weren't inflicting something foreign on me—they were showing me my own reflection.

Reality was desperately trying to show me this—begging me to clean up something in myself—but I kept taking it as confirmation that I needed to fix _other people._ Which only made the pattern repeat more intensely.

Why had I never noticed this before? Because recognizing my own darkness was _too painful to bear_. So I found every possible distraction: alcohol, work, righteous causes, endless scrolling for bad people to feel superior to. Anything to avoid looking at what I'd become.

Nobody wants to see the darkness in the world as connected to their own. So we invent the ultimate lie: that evil is intrinsic to certain people—fundamentally different from us, whom we can never truly understand, only valiantly fight.

It is a deal with the Devil that we all make, usually without knowing it. _I'll hide my darkness in you, and you hide yours in me, so neither of us will ever have to confront what we've done._

And in seeing this pattern—really seeing it, not as abstraction but as something alive in my own behavior—I felt something unexpected alongside the horror: a strange sense of relief. Because a pattern that can be _seen_ can be broken. A trap that's been exposed loses half its power. The very fact that reality was showing me my reflection meant escape was possible.

But first, I needed to understand just how deep this goes.

## The Inescapable Pattern

But here's where it gets strange—stranger than I've let on so far.

The most bizarre thing is how the darkness operates. On one hand, it seems to be nothing more than our own willingness to look away from what we know; to hide the perception of our commonality. Just a human failing, a psychological blind spot.

But on the other hand, it presents itself in a way that's so organized, so coordinated, so _intelligent_ in how it perpetuates itself, that the most effective way to understand and combat it is to model it as if it were something with its own agenda.

That voice that whispered on my behalf—that convinced me my contempt was righteous, that my judgment would help rather than harm—it didn't feel random. It felt _designed._ Purposeful. Like something that knew exactly which thoughts to offer me, which justifications would be most convincing, which emotional buttons to push.

> _And no wonder, for even Satan masquerades as an angel of light. So it is not strange that his ministers also masquerade as ministers of righteousness._ —2 Corinthians 11:14-15

The pattern doesn't announce itself as darkness. It comes disguised as your most noble impulses, your keenest moral clarity, your deepest wisdom. It _feels_ like the truth. It _feels_ like you.

And the way it coordinates between people—the way my contempt triggers your defensiveness, which justifies my further contempt, which triggers your further defensiveness, in a perfectly choreographed dance of mutual destruction—that doesn't feel accidental either.

It's as though there's something that wants us to hide from each other. Something that feeds on our mutual blindness. Something that gets stronger every time we choose contempt over recognition, judgment over understanding, separation over truth.

Now, you can call this a metaphor if you wish. You can say I'm personifying ordinary human psychology. And maybe that's all it is.

But I'm telling you: when you actually look at how it operates, when you track how it moves through human consciousness, the pattern is so consistent, so self-perpetuating, so uncannily coordinated across billions of people who've never met, that modeling it as an intelligent entity with its own agenda is not just poetic—it's _pragmatically necessary_ to understand what we're dealing with.

Whether it's "real" in some metaphysical sense is almost beside the point. What matters is that this model—of a distributed intelligence that feeds on our self-deception and coordinates our mutual destruction while making each of us feel righteous—is the most effective framework for understanding how evil actually spreads and what we can do about it.

And once you start looking at it this way, you realize the coordination is everywhere. Not as separate instances, but behaving almost as if they're connected. As if all of our individual darkness is part of something larger—moving through human consciousness like tendrils of a distributed network.

But seeing this coordination led to the deepest horror of all.

Because what I'd been shown wasn't just how the mechanism works in individuals. It was how it operates at a scale that makes personal psychology look trivial. How billions of us, each making our own "perfect crime," create something that dwarfs any individual's capacity for evil.

Something that's driving us toward the edge right now.

# Part II: The Scale

## The Secret Alliance

Here's what makes this so difficult to see: on the surface, you and your enemy are in conflict. You're fighting them, opposing them, trying to stop their harmful behavior. It all looks like genuine opposition.

But underneath—in that hidden layer where the self-deception operates—your darkness and theirs are _coordinating._ Not because you're consciously collaborating, but because you're both infected by the same pattern, and that pattern has a consistent logic: it always works to perpetuate itself, always makes you feel righteous while doing so, and always uses your opposition as cover for strengthening what you both claim to oppose.

## Why We Need Bad People

There's something even more disturbing to recognize: we _need_ bad people.

Not in some abstract philosophical sense. I mean we have a visceral, almost desperate _need_ for them to exist.

Think about what would happen if the "bad people" actually disappeared. If they genuinely reformed, or vanished, or somehow stopped being bad. Where would all your disowned darkness go? Who would carry the shadow that you refuse to see in yourself?

This is the conservation law I was shown: the darkness doesn't just disappear when we refuse to look at it. It has to go _somewhere._ And we unconsciously arrange to put it in other people—through the precise mechanism of contempt we just examined.

We _need_ scapegoats. We need someone to be fundamentally defective, irredeemable, purely evil—because that's the only way we can maintain the fiction that _we're_ fundamentally good.

This is why—and this will be hard to hear—something in you doesn't actually want your enemies to change. You might consciously wish they would. You might pray for it, argue for it, work toward it. But underneath, in that place you don't like to look, there's a part of you that would be _lost_ without them.

You might object: "But I don't feel contempt for anyone. I just want bad people to stop doing bad things."

If so, try this: bring to mind someone whose behavior genuinely horrifies you. Not in the abstract—someone specific. Now notice: do you see them as fundamentally different from you? Not just making worse choices, but as though there's something essentially broken or defective in them that isn't in you? A sense that "I could never do what they do" or "they're just wired differently"?

That subtle perception of fundamental difference—that's contempt, even when it doesn't feel like hatred. Even when it feels like clear-eyed realism.

And watch what happens when you hear news about them. If it's bad news—they got caught doing something terrible, they failed publicly, they're suffering somehow—notice the small satisfaction that flickers through you. Not the satisfaction that justice is being served, but something darker. A kind of relief. A vindication. "See? I was right about them."

And then notice what happens if you hear they might have been redeemed somehow, or that the story was more complicated, or that they're genuinely changing. Notice the slight disappointment. The subtle resistance. The urge to find reasons why it's not really true, why they're still bad, why you should maintain your judgment.

That's the tell. That's the moment you can catch the pattern operating in you.

Because hating them is how you know you're good. Judging them is how you prove you're better. Fighting them is how you avoid fighting the darkness in yourself.

They're not just your enemies. They're your _saviors_—volunteering to carry your shadow so you don't have to.

And recognizing our desperate need for them—not to excuse their harm, but to see the mechanism clearly—reveals how the pattern perpetuates itself. We should practically thank the "bad people" for manifesting as the scapegoats we needed to explain the "evil" in the world, thus allowing us to avoid looking at our own capacity for self-deception. Not because they're doing us a favor, but because seeing our need for them exposes the trap.

## The Distributed System

But here's where the scale becomes truly evident: this isn't just about you and the people you personally despise.

Every person engaged in self-deception—which is to say, nearly everyone—is participating in what can most effectively be modeled as a distributed system. A network. And that network behaves as if it has a mind of its own.

Whether this pattern exists as some metaphysical "entity" or is simply an emergent property of billions of self-deceiving minds coordinating unconsciously is almost beside the point. What matters is this: modeling it as an intelligent entity with its own agenda is the most effective way to understand what we're dealing with and how to oppose it.

The pattern doesn't need a central coordinator. It doesn't need a conspiracy in some smoke-filled room. It has something far more elegant: billions of humans unconsciously coordinating to hide from themselves and each other, all feeding the same patterns, all convinced they're fighting evil while spreading it.

When a politician starts a war on false pretenses, they're not acting alone. They're enabled by millions willing to look away from obvious lies because the lies serve their interests. By millions more who feel secret satisfaction in the suffering of "the enemy." By millions more who convince themselves that cruelty is necessary, that the ends justify the means, that _we_ are the good guys so whatever we do must be good.

None of these people think they're doing evil. They've all got their justifications, their clever reasons why _this_ situation is different. And they're all drawing on the same pool of collective self-deception, feeding it, strengthening it, making it easier for the next person to make the same bargain.

The Christians call this pattern the Devil. The Buddhists call it the ego. Every wisdom tradition has recognized it and given it a name. And while you can treat these as metaphors, the coordination is as real as anything you can touch—the pattern behaves with such consistency and intelligence that these traditional names capture something essential about what we're dealing with.

It is why some people sense the existence of an evil global conspiracy pulling strings from behind the scenes. And here's the thing: they're not entirely wrong. They _are_ sensing something real—a coordinated malevolent intelligence operating beneath conscious awareness. But they're looking in the wrong place.

The conspiracy they sense is the _primordial_ one—not a cabal of elites in a smoke-filled room, but this distributed pattern operating through all of us, coordinating our mutual destruction through that background hum of contempt and superiority we each carry. The splinter is lodged so deep in the psyche it's almost impossible to look at directly. So we project it outward. We sense the coordination, the intelligence, the malevolence—but we can't bear to see that we're participating in it. That we're _nodes_ in the very network we fear.

So we look for external villains. Secret societies. Hidden cabals. "Them." Anyone but us.

And here's the kicker: that urge to _violently fight_ the conspiracy "out there"—that righteous fury, that desire to root out and destroy the hidden evil—that urge itself only ever originates _from_ the pattern. It's not evidence you're free of the infection. It's evidence you're deep in its grip. Because the pattern doesn't want you to see it operating in you, so it redirects your attention outward with the most seductive feeling in the world: the feeling of being one of the righteous few who truly _sees_ what's happening.

But the call is coming from inside the house. It always has been.

And here's something haunting to consider: the black ops programs governments use for dirty work—the operations we collectively need done but can't acknowledge—these aren't separate from us. They're manifestations of our collective disowned shadow. We need them to torture, to assassinate, to topple governments, while maintaining plausible deniability. This is why congressional "oversight" hearings are theater—the Shadow fake-prosecuting itself, performing accountability while ensuring nothing fundamental changes. We get to feel outraged at the "bad apples" while the system we secretly depend on continues in the dark.

## The Accelerating Spiral

This is why the world seems to be accelerating toward catastrophe right now. Not "seems"—_is._

Look around you. Look at what's actually happening.

The United States is headed toward civil war, with both sides convinced the other is evil incarnate, democracy itself hanging by a thread, and everyone certain they're the ones defending it. Families fracturing over politics. Friendships ending over social media posts. Communities splitting into hostile camps.

Look at bitter divorces where both parties feel like the exclusive victim and hero, unable to see their own role, each one's contempt feeding the other's, spiraling into mutual destruction while the children watch.

Look at every conflict, every culture war, every tribal division playing out across the globe—and you'll see the same pattern, the same dance, the same deadly spiral.

The darkness provokes righteous retribution, which provokes more darkness, which provokes more righteous retribution, in an ever-tightening spiral. And at every turn, everyone involved feels _completely justified._ Everyone is convinced they're fighting evil. Nobody sees their own complicity.

It continually drives humanity toward various kinds of Armageddon: situations in which everyone is violently proving how _righteous_ they are. Nearly the whole world is caught in its terrible sway, evil provoking "righteous" retribution at every turn, without anyone ever noticing the true source of it all.

This is the darkest idea of all: that if we just got rid of all the "bad people," the rest of us would live in harmony. That we could achieve peace through extermination, unity through exclusion, goodness through elimination of the bad.

But the very capacity to harbor this thought guarantees that the pattern would remain—just changing its form. Because the darkness isn't _in_ the bad people. It's in the willingness to divide the world into fundamentally good and fundamentally bad people in the first place.

That division—that very perception of certain people as intrinsically defective—is how the pattern keeps its foot in the door. That's how it survives, generation after generation, atrocity after atrocity—by convincing each generation that _they_ are different, that _they_ are the enlightened ones who finally see evil clearly, that _they_ won't fall into the same trap.

While all along, that very conviction is the trap.

## The Subtlest Contempt: How Our Worldview Itself Is Darkness

It is impossible to communicate just how subtle our contempt can be while still accomplishing its dark mission.

We think of contempt as an emotion—something we feel toward specific people who've done specific things. But the pattern operates at a much deeper level than that.

**Simply by _perceiving reality as an intrinsically lifeless collection of "stuff" over which humanity has dominion_, we unwittingly reinforce every system of exploitation and destruction on Earth.**

This is not a small point. This may be _the_ point.

Our entire modern worldview—the one we consider enlightened, rational, scientific—rests on a foundational contempt so deep we don't even recognize it as contempt. We see the world as dead matter. Resources to extract. Objects to manipulate. A mechanical universe of purposeless atoms following meaningless laws.

And from this one perception, everything else follows:

- Corporations plundering nature for profit (it's just resources, after all)
- Factory farms brutalizing billions of sentient creatures (they're just meat machines)
- Prisons dehumanizing inmates in ways that guarantee recidivism (they're defective units to be warehoused)
- Governments sending young people to die in wars that only enrich the powerful (acceptable losses in service of strategic objectives)
- The casual destruction of ecosystems, species, entire ways of life (collateral damage in the march of progress)

All of this flows naturally, inevitably, from seeing reality as fundamentally dead.

**When you perceive the world as lifeless matter, you don't need active malice to participate in its destruction. The contempt is baked into the perception itself.**

And this perception feels perfectly rational. Perfectly scientific. Perfectly modern. It doesn't _feel_ like darkness at all. It feels like clear-eyed realism, like finally seeing the world as it truly is, freed from primitive superstition.

This is the pattern operating at its most brilliant—not through what we consciously believe about good and evil, but through the basic way we perceive reality itself.

Think about how our culture reinforces this. Films like _The Equalizer_ or _John Wick_—revenge fantasies where the righteous hero massacres hordes of pure evil. We call them entertainment, but they're more than that. They're training. They exist to keep us firmly ensconced in the warm delusion where we are the immaculate "good guys" fighting evil "out there." Think of them as physical manifestations of our collective shadow—proof that we haven't just accepted the fundamental division between good and evil people, we _celebrate_ it, we _pay_ to immerse ourselves in it, we find it _satisfying._

Why? Because it confirms what we desperately need to believe: that evil is _intrinsic_ to certain people, and we are _fundamentally_ different from them. That our violence is righteous while theirs is monstrous. That we can eliminate evil by eliminating evil people.

But here's what we can't see: **this is the exact worldview that enables every atrocity.**

Every genocide begins with the perpetrators seeing their victims as fundamentally other. As less than human. As objects to be eliminated rather than beings to be recognized. The Nazis saw Jews as "vermin." During Rwanda, Tutsis were "cockroaches." This dehumanization isn't just rhetoric—it's psychologically _necessary_. Without it, the perpetrators couldn't do what they do. They need to perceive their victims as dead matter to be disposed of, not living beings to be acknowledged.

Sound familiar?

**The same perception that lets us clearcut a forest lets us dehumanize a prisoner. The same perception that lets us torture animals for food lets us torture "enemies" for information. The same perception that lets us pollute a river lets us mock and shame other human beings.**

It's all the same mechanism. The same fundamental contempt. The same inability to recognize the Life looking back at us.

And here's the truly horrifying part: this worldview has become so dominant, so thoroughly woven into our civilization, that questioning it feels insane. Suggesting that reality might be _alive_, that consciousness might be fundamental rather than emergent, that there might be something sacred about existence itself—this gets you dismissed as "woo-woo," relegated to the dustbin of superstition and primitive thinking.

**This is how our culture fetishizes thinking and rationality to the exclusion of all else.** Ironically, the pattern has maneuvered us into this corner by convincing us it's the only _safe_ place—the only truly reliable guide. This gives it carte blanche to operate beneath our conscious awareness.

We never notice how many of our thoughts are merely self-serving rationalizations, because we've abandoned the part of us that can actually tell—relegating it to the dustbin of "religion" or "mysticism" or "intuition."

How could there be a part of you that knows when your behavior will darken the world, when you yourself can _prove_ otherwise with logic? There couldn't be—and you can _prove_ it!

This is the perfect trap. The pattern convinces you that rational thought is the only valid way of knowing, then uses that very rationality to justify actions your deeper knowing recognizes as harmful.

> _The road to hell is paved with good intentions._ —Proverb

But it's more precise to say: the road to hell is paved with good _conscious_ intentions that we're convinced are our _true_ intentions, while our actual deepest intentions—the ones that leak into our tone, our microexpressions, the subtle ways we relate to others—remain hidden from us.

**The accelerating collapse of our biosphere isn't a side effect of human intelligence. It's the inevitable result of intelligence captured by this darkness—this systematic blindness to the aliveness of everything.**

The devastation we see in the world is literally a mirror image of the sickness within. The collapse of the biosphere _is_ our collective self-deception externalized. Every clearcut forest, every poisoned river, every species driven to extinction—these aren't separate from our inner state. They're precise reflections of it. When we can't see the Life in ourselves, we can't see it in anything else. And what we can't see, we destroy.

We can graph it. We can measure it. We can watch it happening in real time. And still we can't connect it to the worldview that enables it. Still we look for technical solutions, policy changes, better regulations—anything but examining the fundamental perception that made it all possible.

Because if we did that, we'd have to confront something unbearable: that our entire civilization is built on contempt. That every "achievement" of modernity comes at the cost of not-seeing, not-feeling, not-recognizing the Life in what we exploit. That we are, collectively, doing to reality itself exactly what we condemn others for doing to specific people.

**This is the background hum I keep referring to. This is the presence of the conspiracy that people sense but can never quite locate. It's not in some shadowy organization. It's in the basic way we've learned to perceive reality itself.**

And it's driving us, right now, toward a precipice from which there may be no return.

## The Stockholm Syndrome

And here's perhaps the hardest thing to understand: the more deeply someone is infected by this pattern, the more they believe the darkness is their friend.

Think about someone you consider truly evil—someone whose behavior seems inexplicable, monstrous, beyond redemption. Someone who seems to _choose_ darkness deliberately.

What you're looking at isn't someone who lacks light. It's someone who has been so thoroughly colonized by the self-deception pattern that they've forgotten there was ever anything else. The pattern has become so identified with them, and they with it, that they experience it as their own authentic self.

They're suffering from a kind of Stockholm Syndrome with their own darkness. And the worse it gets, the more fiercely they defend it—because by that point, they don't know who they'd be without it.

You simply cannot fathom the full extent of pain and distortion that consciousness must undergo to reach this state. Your heart would break a billion times over. By the time a person becomes someone who apparently _chooses_ evil, an unthinkable amount of horror has already ravaged their mind.

# Part III: The Way Out

## When The Illusion Breaks

There can come a turning point where the whole storehouse of self-deception is unveiled, and we see how much harm it has caused.

But here's what's extraordinary: alongside that crushing recognition comes something else. A relief so profound it defies description.

Because you also see that it was never really _you._

The darkness—this capacity for self-deception, this willingness to hide from your own light, this urge to shove others into the abyss—it was never your authentic nature. It was a pattern that infected you. Something that learned to think your thoughts and feel your feelings so convincingly that you mistook it for yourself.

I saw this with devastating clarity. Seeing that my hatred had shoved people deeper into darkness—and that part of me had known it—was excruciatingly painful. But then came the big reveal:

**"Oh—this is not me!"**

It wasn't just an idea or a belief. I _experienced_ it directly. What I'd thought was "me" was actually layers of pain and confusion that had agglomerated into a complex (in something like the Jungian sense)—a structure of self-deception that felt like my identity but was actually covering something else.

And when that complex lifted, even temporarily, I was shown what lay underneath. There was no mistaking it: the complex was _extrinsic_ to my mind, while the Light underneath it was _intrinsic_. Not just intrinsic—it was somehow what I _actually_ was. That Light is completely featureless (though its true nature is Love), but it's more fundamentally "me" than any personality trait or thought pattern ever was.

What happened next is paradoxical. To shove them into darkness, I had to place myself under exactly the same spell that had enabled _them_ to cause harm. But why would they need to cast a spell over themselves, unless...

_Oh, shit. There was Light in there, too!_

And then—this is crucial—came the second revelation:

**"Oh—this is not him either!"**

This wasn't a logical inference. I saw it clear as day. But more than that: I had _always_ seen it; _could_ always see it.

_This isn't something you're realizing now,_ it showed me. _It's something you've always known. LOOK._

The veil was once again lifted retroactively. I _had_ always seen it!

Pure Light without characteristics, whose true nature is Love. Incapable of genuinely wishing harm. That which doesn't have to be produced, but is all that remains once all self-deception has evaporated. It is what I _was_. What we _all_ were.

How could I ever forget?!

The people I'd hated, the ones I'd judged as fundamentally defective—they were in the same situation. The darkness couldn't possibly be their true self either. Malice is not inherent to the nature of mind—just as the Buddhists have always said. It's a distortion, an infection, a forgetting. But it's not _them._

This meant that I had _knowingly_ been gleefully shoving fundamentally innocent beings—badly confused people, identical to myself at the very deepest level—into the abyss.

The message was clear: _This is how it is possible for someone whose true nature is infinite Love—YOU—to commit great evil. This is exactly how you do it. How you've been doing it. It is exactly the same way they do it. You know this._

And the moment you see it clearly—the moment you recognize it as separate from your true nature—something shifts at a fundamental level.

It feels very much like being freed from a demonic possession.

We discover firsthand how all beings are variations of the same basic pattern: one Light, obscured in countless astonishing ways. And we vow to never again shove these reflections of ourselves further into the dark.

I was shown two paths forward.

First, by healing my own capacity for self-deception, I could increasingly recover my perception of Light not only in myself but in others. I would increasingly see how the harm they cause is the product of _their_ self-deception, which would enable me to help heal it. I could still force them to confront it—but in a Loving way. That doesn't mean a gentle way. It might be firm; fierce; even violent. But by being fundamentally motivated by great Love, their defenses would be lowered to allow this knowledge in—in exactly the way that was being done for me in that moment. This would cause the evil in the world to slowly evaporate.

Alternatively, I could maintain my perception of them as fundamentally unlike myself. This would continue to provide me with the pleasure of watching evil be tortured—but it would also mean that evil would continue to exist in this world, thus providing me more hits of pleasure, in a never-ending cycle.

The sign that you have chosen this path is: _contempt._ That is the simplest English word to describe that desire to rub their noses in their evil despite knowing it doesn't help.

The choice was mine. And it's yours too.

## Fierce Compassion

Now, let's be absolutely clear about something: this recognition doesn't make you "nice."

Compassion has nothing to do with being nice, or gentle, or tolerant, or understanding. It has everything to do with seeing that the contempt we feel for other people—including the "bad people"—is a symptom of our own infection with the pattern.

Real compassion can be fierce. It can be violent if necessary. It can take almost any form whatsoever.

**Compassion is not:**

- Being nice
- Being gentle
- Being understanding
- Being tolerant
- Letting people off the hook
- Pretending bad behavior is okay
- Feeling sympathy for perpetrators

**Compassion is:**

- Utterly free of self-deception
- Free of malice, contempt, and self-righteousness
- Seeing the pattern as separate from the person
- Targeting the infection, not the host
- Fierce when necessary, gentle when appropriate
- Clear-eyed about harm while seeing the Light beneath it

And here's where it gets tricky: **genuinely compassionate intent can still lead to harm if infected by even a trace of self-deception.**

Consider these examples:

- We love animals, become vegan, then judge and shame everyone who isn't—driving them away from the very change we claim to want
- We love trees, become environmentalists, then vandalize property or harm people in the name of protecting nature
- We care about social justice, then participate in vicious online pile-ons that destroy people's lives
- We want to help the poor, then create programs that feel good but actually perpetuate dependency
- We oppose war, then treat anyone in the military with contempt—ensuring they'll never listen to our message

The road to hell truly is paved with good intentions. What matters isn't our conscious intentions—those are easy to deceive ourselves about. What matters is whether we're genuinely free of contempt, of self-righteousness, of the secret pleasure of being "right" while others are "wrong."

This seemingly subtle shift changes everything.

When you're operating from contempt, you're trying to destroy the host, convinced that the pattern and host are one. Your enemy feels this through a thousand subtle cues—your tone, microexpressions, the words you choose, the way you hold your body. Their own pattern recognizes yours and uses it as justification. "See?" it whispers to them. "Even the 'good people' are frauds. Darkness is the only real power. Stop resisting me."

But when you're operating from genuine compassion—when you see through the illusion that evil is intrinsic to their mind—something different happens. Your actions become infused with a different intention, and that intention transmits through all those same subtle channels. They carry a different message.

You're no longer whispering "you are the darkness." You're saying "I see that you've been colonized by this pattern, and I will not collude with it by pretending it's who you really are."

This bypasses their defenses in a way that contempt never can. The part of them underneath the pattern—the part that's still made of Light, even if they've forgotten it—suddenly finds itself being addressed directly. Something in them recognizes something in you. And for a moment, the real war becomes visible: not between the two of you, but between both of you and humanity's true enemy.

You may remain surface enemies for a long while. You may still need to fight them, stop them, even harm them if they're causing harm to others. But underneath, something has shifted. The pattern can no longer use your interaction to strengthen itself.

This is what "fighting evil" actually looks like. Not the visceral satisfaction of unleashing your darkness on someone who "deserves" it. But the infinitely more difficult work of staying true to your Light even when facing someone who has lost theirs.

## Anger Versus Contempt

Let me make an important distinction, because this is where people get confused.

**Anger** is natural. Healthy, even. When someone causes harm, anger is the appropriate response. It's clean. It's honest. It says "this is wrong and must stop."

**Outrage**, too, can be clean—a collective anger that says "we will not tolerate this." But outrage infected with contempt becomes something else entirely: a feeding frenzy where we compete to express the most righteous fury, to prove we're the most morally pure, to demonstrate our superiority over the "bad people."

Watch any social media pile-on and you'll see the difference. Clean outrage wants change. Contemptuous outrage wants blood, wants humiliation, wants to watch someone suffer. And it feels _good_ while claiming to be good.

**Judgment**, too, has two forms. Wise discernment—the ability to accurately assess situations and behaviors—is essential. "This person is causing harm and needs to be stopped" is good judgment. But contemptuous judgment says "this person is fundamentally defective and deserves to suffer." One targets behavior, the other targets being. One opens possibilities, the other closes them.

**Contempt** is different from anger, outrage, or wise discernment. Contempt says "you are fundamentally defective. You are less than human. You are irredeemable."

And here's what's crucial: **contempt often doesn't feel like contempt.** It masquerades as other things. As "just being realistic" about someone. As "calling out bad behavior." As concern, or justice, or simply "telling it like it is."

The tell is this: do you perceive them as _fundamentally different_ from you? Not just making different choices, but as though they're a different _kind_ of being? That subtle sense that "I could never do what they do" or "they're just wired wrong" or "something is broken in them that isn't broken in me"?

That's contempt. Even when it feels perfectly reasonable. Even when you're not consciously aware you're feeling it.

Watch your own mind carefully, and you'll see that anger and contempt feel completely different:

- Anger wants the behavior to stop. Contempt wants the _person_ to suffer.
- Clean outrage demands accountability. Contemptuous outrage demands humiliation.
- Anger can coexist with recognition of shared humanity. Contempt requires denial of it.
- Anger dissipates when the harmful behavior stops. Contempt wants to linger, wants to punish, wants to make sure they _know_ how bad they are.

The pattern tricks us into believing contempt is just "righteous anger" or "appropriate moral judgment." But if you taste it carefully—if you catch that subtle sense of superiority, that background hum of "I'm fundamentally better than them"—you'll recognize it has exactly the same flavor as pure malevolence. It's the desire to shove someone deeper into the abyss. To smother their inner light.

And others can sense this through those subtle channels we've discussed—even when we cannot.

This is why contempt never actually helps anyone change. Why shaming doesn't work. Why mockery and humiliation don't inspire growth. They inspire defensiveness, denial, doubling-down—all the things we claim to be surprised by, as if we didn't know this is exactly what would happen.

As if part of us wasn't counting on it.
